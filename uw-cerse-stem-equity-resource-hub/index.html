UW CERSE - UW SERCH (STEM Equity Resource Community Hub)

A virtual extension of the UW LSAMP Center

For my HCDE Senior Capstone, my team and I partnered with the UW Center for Evaluation & Research for STEM Equity, a research center. The focus of our capstone project was to produce an interactive platform to connect students, staff, and faculty to campus resources supporting equitable student success in STEM.

Client Context

Who are we partnering with?

The UW Center for Evaluation & Research for STEM Equity, or UW CERSE, is a team of researchers who focus on conducting research to improve equity and broaden representation in STEM fields in higher education. Their current research is focusing on the PNW LSAMP (Pacific Northwest Louis Stokes Alliance for Minority Participation), a university program consisting of several universities and community colleges from Washington, Oregon, and Idaho whose mission is to support underrepresented students pursuing a bachelor's degree in STEM.

From their research, they've observed that efforts related to improving STEM equity are often siloed, hard to locate, and sometimes provide gaps in the types of support that students need. Our team's task was to learn about where these gaps occur, and alleviate this issue by creating an interactive STEM resource hub platform.

Design Question

How might we design a platform that encourages students and staff to take advantage of the various STEM resources on campus, and make finding these opportunities easier and seamless?

Product Deliverable

A seamless way to locate STEM resources

Extensive research, ideation, and testing brought us to our developed resource hub website prototype, as showcased in this product video.

UW SERCH (STEM Equity Resource Community Hub) is a virtual extension of the UW LSAMP Center, spotlighting STEM opportunities, events, and resources, while creating opportunity for interpersonnal connections and communication between staff and students.

UW SERCH product video

Our impact

By using UW SERCH, students can find research opportunities, get academic support, and connect with their peers. Staff are able to foster interpersonal relationships with students, improving student advising efforts and building community. There is also the opportunity for facilitated collaboration between efforts from various campus organizations to increase equity in STEM.

While the development of the STEM resource hub is still in progress, our work was able to provide UW CERSE with more insight regarding the types of resources and opportunities that LSAMP students and staff want to see in a resource hub, as well as give direction for further iterations of a more-detailed platform by expanding the scope to include the other schools that are part of the PNW LSAMP. Our hope is that the UW SERCH platform will flourish into a go-to resource for students and staff to find information about the various efforts on campus that were created to help support their academic success.

Deep diving into our problem space

To familiarize ourself with our project, our team first conducted literature review to explore the domains relevant to our problem space to discover who is historically marginalized in STEM and understand the roadblocks to finding STEM resources. Prior to beginning our resesarch, our team consulted with our sponsor on project direction, in which they recommended staff at the PNW LSAMP centers for us to connect with so we could get a better understanding on what the LSAMP is. Our team was able to connect directly with the LSAMP coordinators at UW, where we had the opportunity to visit the UW LSAMP center to get a better sense of what resources exist in the physical space.

Participants and Recruitment

Our team wrote up and sent out a Google Forms survey to student mailing lists provided by LSAMP coordinators for the five institutions in the Pacific Northwest region. Additionally, surveys were sent out to several University of Washington STEM-related student organizations such as SWE (Society of Women Engineers), WiC (Women in Computing), and MiT (Minorities in Tech). In total, we received 11 responses.

Survey Procedure

Our survey started with demographic questions for our team to have a better understanding of participants' backgrounds and how that may impact their experiences, as well as ensure that diverse perspectives were being included as part of our research. After the demographics, we included a variety of both qualitative and quantitative questions. Survey results were then analyzed using the analytics from the Google Form Responses section.

Research: Interviews

Getting a clearer picture on the direction

To understand our research goals, our team also conducted interviews. Our team interviewed LSAMP staff as well as some students that had filled out our survey indicating they were interested in participating in a follow-up interview. Our interviews allowed our team to get more insight on the findings from our survey.

Participants and Recruitment

Our team conducted a total of 4 interviews (2 students and 2 staff). For each interview, we had two team members sit in, with one facilitator and one notetaker. We decided to include staff in our interview pool as we had only gotten responses from students in our survey, and we wanted to see if staff and students had aligned experiences regarding the accessibility of STEM resources.

Interview Procedure

For our interviews, we focused on accessibility of resources and if there are any challenges they encounter when looking for STEM resources. After our interviews were conducted, our team analyzed our interview transcripts by developing a codebook to identifying patterns and themes in our data.

Research: Competitive Analysis

Looking at our competitors for inspiration

We analyzed existing platforms and student organizations, looking at the accessibility of the platform/student organization (the effectiveness of their utilization of technology platforms, such as websites or mobile apps, to share STEM resources), interactivity with other users within the community, and intuitiveness to navigate the platform.

Platforms & Organizations

We identified existing platforms and student organizations that function similar to our envisioned platform that we were developing for our project, in which they provide STEM resources and opportunities for students. We analyzed a total of 4 platforms and 4 student organizations.

Competitive Analysis Procedure

We noted each respective platform/organization's strengths and weaknesses. We then synthesized our findings into a cohesive list and noted the strong points we could add to our platform. We also took notice of common weaknesses that cause pain points for students as they are using and interacting with these platforms and organizations.

Highlighted Research Findings

What did we learn?

Mode of technology

Students indicated that a website is their most utilized and preferred medium for accessing resources. Some students stated that a mobile app feels limiting, and they mainly access websites such as Indeed, Handshake, and the UW website, through desktop.

Website platform

Use a website as a virtual extension of the LSAMP center. A website acts as the centralized location for STEM resources to be located and shared.

Outdated resources

Students and staff specified that one of the challenges they face is that many resources are not updated in current time. This prevents them from being able to get up-to-date information, and they may end up missing out on opportunities as a result.

Updated and time-logged resources

Date log of updates on the platform indicates the information is accurate and up-to-date.

Unclear POC

When searching for STEM resources, students and staff faced issues when they had no way to contact the person who published the resource online - with no listed contact information, there was no way to learn more about the resource on the resource they were seeking out.

Transparent contact information

Include clear contact information about who/where the resource came from to provide a POC if any questions arise.

Accessibility issues

Students had issues with resources being flat-out hard to find, causing them to go down a rabbit hole during their searches. This created frustration as they were unable to get the exact information they wanted and needed.

Filtering feature

Organize searches in a more conclusive and effective manner.

Community building

Students and staff expressed desire for interpersonal relationships, which would enhance social engagement and provide accountability.

Messaging feature / online social groups

Cultivate mentorship and foster connections within groups through community forums and messaging features.

Motivation

Students and staff emphasized the importance of highlighting students' diverse backgrounds, goals, and apsirations while seeking out STEM-related resources.

Tailored profile

Personalized profile page to cross-check resources being sought, fulfilling the unique desires and needs of each user.

Are there any usability roadblocks?

Once our wireframes were created, we added some interactivity components to link the screens together into user flows. We conducted usability testing with 8 participants to gather insight on the intuitiveness and usability of our website platform prototype.

findings

- Left bar navigation made it easy to understand the functionality of each tab - Filtering had some bugs but this could be due to the interactivity of the prototype - Some participants had difficulty as we had mapped the path to only work once the filter arrow was closed or opened - The search bar was often the first line of action to find an organization or event - There are many different avenues to do the same thing which could be confusing 12 - The landing screen could be cluttered since there are many buttons to click - The biggest issue is the clickability and flow of the wireframe, not really the design - Wording of certain buttons, especially in filtering, is not specific enough - Introduce a feature to recommend events and opportunities to other students - Personalization in the opportunities and resources shown would be helpful (i.e. specific internships or events based on their interests or major)

Reflecting and looking to the future

Overall, this 6-month project was a significant learning experience as I was able to work on an end-to-end project. It was rewarding to look back and see how our project started from a paragraph on the project context to a website prototype after extensive research, analysis, and ideation.

  • communication is key! As the team member in charge of heading communications with UW CERSE, it was imperative that I handled deadlines and kept our sponsor in loop on our status and roadblocks.
  • Flexibility in times of uncertainty. Our team ran into difficulties when recruiting participants for our surveys and interviews. Fortunately, we were able to widen our participant pool through our sponsor and personal connections.

If we had more time, I would definitely want to test the high-fidelity prototype with further user testing. I would also want to have more input from staff perspective as our project mainly focused on student-view - fleshing out the staff user flow would allow more insight on if their needs are being met. Some questions I would explore are:

  • How intuitive to users find the filtering?
  • Are there any STEM resources missing?
  • How would further implementation of this project go when broadening the scope to include the other schools in the PNW LSAMP?
  • Can we observe success metrics such as increase in engagement rate of students accessing STEM resources?
  • Are the features and designs feasible to implement from a developer standpoint?
