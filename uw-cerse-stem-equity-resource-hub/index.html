UW CERSE - UW SERCH (STEM Equity Resource Community Hub)

A virtual extension of the UW LSAMP Center

For my HCDE Senior Capstone, my team and I partnered with UW CERSE (UW Center for Evaluation & Research for STEM Equity), a research center at the University of Washington, Seattle. The focus of our capstone project was to produce an interactive platform to connect students, staff, and faculty to campus resources supporting equitable student success in STEM.

Client Context

Who are we partnering with?

Map of PNW LSAMP

The UW Center for Evaluation & Research for STEM Equity, or UW CERSE, is a team of researchers who focus on conducting research to improve equity and broaden representation in STEM fields in higher education. Their current research is focusing on the PNW LSAMP (Pacific Northwest Louis Stokes Alliance for Minority Participation), a university program consisting of several universities and community colleges from Washington, Oregon, and Idaho whose mission is to support underrepresented students pursuing a bachelor's degree in STEM.

From their research, they've observed that efforts related to improving STEM equity are often siloed, hard to locate, and sometimes provide gaps in the types of support that students need. Our team's task was to learn about where these gaps occur, and alleviate this issue by creating an interactive STEM resource hub platform.

Design Question

How might we design a platform that encourages students and staff to take advantage of the various STEM resources on campus, and make finding these opportunities easier and seamless?

Product Deliverable

A seamless way to locate STEM resources

Extensive research, ideation, and testing brought us to our developed resource hub website prototype, as showcased in this product video.

UW SERCH (STEM Equity Resource Community Hub) is a virtual extension of the UW LSAMP Center, spotlighting STEM opportunities, events, and resources, while creating opportunity for interpersonnal connections and communication between staff and students.

UW SERCH product video

Our impact

By using UW SERCH, students can find research opportunities, get academic support, and connect with their peers. Staff are able to foster interpersonal relationships with students, improving student advising efforts and building community. There is also the opportunity for facilitated collaboration between efforts from various campus organizations to increase equity in STEM.

While the development of the STEM resource hub is still in progress, our work was able to provide UW CERSE with more insight regarding the types of resources and opportunities that LSAMP students and staff want to see in a resource hub, as well as give direction for further iterations of a more-detailed platform by expanding the scope to include the other schools that are part of the PNW LSAMP. Our hope is that the UW SERCH platform will flourish into a go-to resource for students and staff to find information about the various efforts on campus that were created to help support their academic success.

Overarching Research Goals

1. Understand what types of STEM resources and opportunities users are interested in

What constitutes as a STEM 'resource'?

What resources and opportunities do students and staff look for?

2. Understand users' current comfort level with finding and sharing STEM resources

What methods do students and staff use to find resources?

How do staff share resources with students?

3. Understand roadblocks to finding STEM resources

How accessible is it to locate STEM resources?

Deep diving into our problem space

To familiarize ourself with our project, our team first conducted literature review to explore the domains relevant to our problem space to discover who is historically marginalized in STEM and understand the roadblocks to finding STEM resources. Prior to beginning our resesarch, our team consulted with our sponsor on project direction, in which they recommended staff at the PNW LSAMP centers for us to connect with so we could get a better understanding on what the LSAMP is. Our team was able to connect directly with the LSAMP coordinators at UW, where we had the opportunity to visit the UW LSAMP center to get a better sense of what resources exist in the physical space.

Research: Surveys

The 'who' and the 'what'

Our team wrote up and sent out a survey to understand Research Goals #2 and #3. We also wanted to see what STEM resources students and staff look for, what methods are used to access STEM resources, how accessible resources are, and what features they would like to see in a resource hub platform.

Who

Our survey was sent out to students at universities in the PNW LSAMP. Our team received a total of 11 responses from students of various years, majors, and universities.

Method of accessing

81.8% of participants access resources through the web over mobile apps and word of mouth. A website could be the most useful medium for a STEM resource hubs platform.

Accessibility of resources

About half of participants rated the accessibility of STEM resources as a 3, which is a 'C' grade - just okay but can do better. This supports the purpose of our capstone project, as we are seeking to make the experience of finding STEM resources more efficient, seamless, and accessible.

What

Survey questions included a variety of qualitative and quantitative questions to:

  • gauge the accessiblility of STEM resources provided to students
  • see what resources staff and students look for
  • uncover how staff and students find resources

Participants and Recruitment

Our team wrote up and sent out a Google Forms survey to student mailing lists provided by LSAMP coordinators for the five institutions in the Pacific Northwest region. Additionally, surveys were sent out to several University of Washington STEM-related student organizations such as SWE (Society of Women Engineers), WiC (Women in Computing), and MiT (Minorities in Tech). In total, we received 11 responses from students in universities across the PNW LSAMP.

Survey Procedure

Our survey started with demographic questions for our team to have a better understanding of participants' backgrounds and how that may impact their experiences, as well as ensure that diverse perspectives were being included as part of our research. After the demographics, we included a variety of both qualitative and quantitative questions. Survey results were then analyzed using the analytics from the Google Form Responses section.

Research: Interviews

Getting a clearer picture on the direction

To understand our research goals, our team also conducted interviews to dig deeper on the findings from the survey and see if there were any commonalities and differences. Our team interviewed LSAMP staff as well as a couple students that had filled out our survey indicating they were interested in participating in a follow-up interview.

Interview Themes & Codes

Based on the analysis of the interview notes, 5 main themes emerged:

  1. User profile
  2. Issues with current resources
  3. Methods to find resources
  4. Suggestions for solution
  5. How resources are used

Interview Codebook

We categorized the main takeaways from all our interview sessions to generate key concepts for our final solution. Under the 5 overarching themes, we also created several sub-categories/codes to help with grouping.

Participants and Recruitment

Our team conducted a total of 4 interviews (2 students and 2 staff). We decided to include staff in our interview pool as we had only gotten responses from students in our survey, and we wanted to see if staff and students had aligned experiences regarding the accessibility of STEM resources.

Interview Procedure

Each interview was conducted on Zoom, and we had two team members sit in, with one facilitator and one notetaker. For our interviews, we focused on accessibility of resources and if there are any challenges they encounter when looking for STEM resources. After our interviews were conducted, our team analyzed our interview transcripts by developing a codebook to identifying patterns and themes in our data.

Research: Competitive Analysis

Looking at our competitors for inspiration

We analyzed existing platforms and student organizations, looking at the accessibility of the platform/student organization (the effectiveness of their utilization of technology platforms, such as websites or mobile apps, to share STEM resources), interactivity with other users within the community, and intuitiveness to navigate the platform.

Platforms & Organizations

We identified existing platforms and student organizations that function similar to our envisioned platform that we were developing for our project, in which they provide STEM resources and opportunities for students. We analyzed a total of 4 platforms and 4 student organizations.

Competitive Analysis Procedure

We noted each respective platform/organization's strengths and weaknesses. We then synthesized our findings into a cohesive list and noted the strong points we could add to our platform. We also took notice of common weaknesses that cause pain points for students as they are using and interacting with these platforms and organizations.

Competitive analysis Miro board

Competitive Analysis

From our analysis of existing platforms and student organizations, we compiled a list of strengths and weaknesses.

Strengths:

The platforms and student organizations focused on celebrating the diversity of their audience, creating space for social engagement, networking, and collaboration.

Weaknesses:

Patterns of requiring membership fees, outdated events calendars, weak filtering features, and tacky thumbnails/design are things we want to improve through our platform.

Strengths

  • Real people sharing their experience (almost review style)
  • Can be accessed by mobile or desktop
  • Messaging feature / community forum for social networking
  • Focus on diversity and inclusion so the community is smaller and niche
  • Users can make a profile to save jobs and tailor their professional online presence

Weaknesses

  • Have to be a member or pay a membership fee to participate
  • Events and opportunity calendards are not updated on a regular basis
  • Difficult to scroll through events page due to weak filtering feature
  • Use of thumbnails and tacky design take away from the aesthetics of the platform

Highlighted Research Findings

What did we learn?

Mode of technology

Students indicated that a website is their most utilized and preferred medium for accessing resources. Some students stated that a mobile app feels limiting, and they mainly access websites such as Indeed, Handshake, and the UW website, through desktop.

Website platform

Use a website as a virtual extension of the LSAMP center. A website acts as the centralized location for STEM resources to be located and shared.

Outdated resources

Students and staff specified that one of the challenges they face is that many resources are not updated in current time. This prevents them from being able to get up-to-date information, and they may end up missing out on opportunities as a result.

Updated and time-logged resources

Date log of updates on the platform indicates the information is accurate and up-to-date.

Unclear POC

When searching for STEM resources, students and staff faced issues when they had no way to contact the person who published the resource online - with no listed contact information, there was no way to learn more about the resource on the resource they were seeking out.

Transparent contact information

Include clear contact information about who/where the resource came from to provide a POC if any questions arise.

Accessibility issues

Students had issues with resources being flat-out hard to find, causing them to go down a rabbit hole during their searches. This created frustration as they were unable to get the exact information they wanted and needed.

Filtering feature

Organize searches in a more conclusive and effective manner.

Community building

Students and staff expressed desire for interpersonal relationships, which would enhance social engagement and provide accountability.

Messaging feature / online social groups

Cultivate mentorship relationships and foster connections within groups through community forums and messaging features.

Motivation

Students and staff emphasized the importance of highlighting students' diverse backgrounds, goals, and apsirations while seeking out STEM-related resources.

Tailored profile

Personalized profile page to let users select which resources they are interested in.

After our research and analysis, our team got in discussion with UW CERSE on project alignment. We decided that going forwards into the next phase of the project (prototyping the resource hub platform), our team would focus solely on the University of Washington due to time constraints for the capstone project. For future implementation of the resource hub, other schools will be considered.

Ideation: Brainstorm & Wireframes

Letting the ideas flow

Our team generated a list of ideas and features to address our users' needs and pain points that we discovered in our research. From our ideation, we clustered our ideas into the following features:

Iteration: Usability Testing

Are there any usability roadblocks?

Interactivity components were added to link the wireframed screens together into user flows. These flows then underwent usability testing to gauge the intuitiveness of the flows and functional usability of the website.

Participants and Recruitment

Our team tested with a total of 8 participants. We sought to test with the students that would be using this website platform, so we recruited our pool from this recruitment criteria:

  • Currently attend the University of Washington
  • Study a STEM major
  • Identify as a minority individual

Usability Testing Procedure

Each test session was conducted in-person or on Zoom, and lasted approximately 20 minutes long, consisting of the completion of two tasks and pre-test & post-task questionnaires. All test sessions were moderated, with one member facilitating the session as the moderator and another team member taking notes. Participants were asked to complete these series of tasks while thinking out loud:

  1. Search for an organization and message their point of contact
  2. Filter and register for an event

Usability test data analysis

Notes from the usability test sessions were grouped by task, focusing on where participants struggled to complete an action or had difficulty with the interface, what features they liked, and any suggestions for improvements. Regarding the website prototype overall, participants felt that the left tab-bar navigation was pretty intuitive and user-friendly due to similarity with other websites they've used before. However, some participants faced challenges due to the visual layout of the website, and there was also some confusion in the wording of certain buttons, specifically during filtering.

Challenge #1

Cluttered landing page

Having many buttons and text on the landing screen created confusion, causing some participants to be overwhelmed as they did not know where to go first.

Updated Design

Simplified landing page view

Reduce clutter on the landing page to lessen the cognitive load. Remove most of the text, replacing it with visuals to make actions more straightforward.

Challenge #2

Unclear button language

There was some confusion on certain button actions, specifically in Events filtering, as the wording was unclear.

Updated Design

Intentional button language

Reword the ambiguous buttons with specific language matching their actions, aiding in decision making for users.

Challenge #3

Lacking tailoring

Participants suggested a helpful feature for finding STEM resources would be to show resources on the landing page based on their profile interests.

Updated Design

Personalization unique to each user

Display specific resources, such as internships, events, etc., on the landing page as 'Recommended for You' based on the user's interests or major.

Iteration: Lo-Fi to High-Fi Prototyping

Putting it all together

Based on feedback and recommendations from the testing, corresponding updates were made to the prototype. Brand color, images, detailed-components, and additional interactivity were implemented to transition to a higher-fidelity prototype. However, due to limited time, our team was unable to recruit and schedule additional usability testing for the high-fidelity prototype. Thus, we were unable to get feedback on and further refine our high-fidelity prototype before handing off to UW CERSE.

Reflecting and looking to the future

If we had more time, I would definitely want to test the high-fidelity prototype with further user testing. I would also want to have more input from staff perspective as our project had mainly focused on student-view - fleshing out the staff user flow would allow more insight on if there are any features we missed. Some questions I would want to explore are:

  • How intuitive do users find the filtering?
  • Are there any STEM resources missing?
  • How would further implementation of this project when broadening the scope to include the other schools in the PNW LSAMP?
  • Can we observe success metrics such as increase in engagement rate of students accessing STEM resources?
  • Are the features and designs feasible to implement from a developer standpoint?

Key Takeaways:

  • Communication is key! As the team member in charge of heading communications with UW CERSE, it was imperative that I handled deadlines and kept our sponsor in loop on our status and roadblocks.
  • Flexibility in times of uncertainty. Our team ran into difficulties when recruiting participants for our surveys and interviews. Fortunately, we were able to widen our participant pool through our sponsor and personal connections.

Overall, this 6-month project was an incredibly rewarding learning experience as I had the opportunity to work on an end-to-end project. It was super cool to look back and see how our project started from a concept idea our sponsor had to a website prototype after many months of extensive research, analysis, and ideation.
